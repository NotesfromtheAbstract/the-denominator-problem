## Working Title: TBD
### Thesis: Men are the underserved population in American healthcare, and the infrastructure to change that does not exist.

### MOVEMENT 1: HOW I GOT HERE

**Entry point:** Personal, not political. The piece arrives through Andrew's own reading, forwarded op-eds, and professional instinct.

- Since "The Engine and the Fuel" published, readers have been forwarding op-eds (or, more precisely, forwarding them in case he wants to respond).
- Russell's USA Today piece ("The left fueled the male crisis. Now it's shocked.") landed in the pile. Her thesis: feminism and left-wing cultural messaging devalued men, and now everyone is surprised they're struggling.
- Agree with much of what she said. Men are by the wayside when it comes to media messaging and campaigns. The data she cites (suicide rate four times higher, college completion falling behind, addiction rates elevated) is not wrong.
- But Andrew's mind goes to healthcare, not culture war. That's where it goes these days.

**The Nightbirde moment:**
- Following that thread led to the Nightbirde Foundation page. Jane Marczewski's voice is achingly beautiful and her story doubly so.
- Full record stop: the message on the foundation page disturbed him. "No Woman Should Face Cancer Alone." The programming, the grants, the care packages — exclusively for women with breast cancer.
- Do men not deal with the same problems? Do men not face cancer alone?
- Not an attack on the foundation. The sentiment is genuine. The absence of anything equivalent is what stopped him.

**The academic thread:**
- Andrew's minor in English (almost a double major, but not that much of a glutton for punishment) included studying feminist writing alongside gender studies.
- Gender studies, as a discipline, identified and criticized feminist studies for the assumption that women always came out on the losing side.
- His female professor said, to considerable backlash from the students, that each gender is "equal in their misery."
- That framing — equal in their misery, different in the shape of it — is the lens for the healthcare argument that follows.
- Russell looked at the misery and blamed feminism. The healthcare data tells a different story about where the misery is concentrated, who built the systems that produced it, and whose suffering has infrastructure addressing it.

### MOVEMENT 2: WHAT YOU SEE WHEN YOU LOOK

**The visible asymmetry in health promotion messaging.**

The NFL pink campaign:
- The league's audience skews overwhelmingly male.
- The platform promotes breast cancer awareness, which already has the most funded, most visible advocacy infrastructure in oncology.
- That platform has never been used to tell the men watching to get screened for prostate cancer, colorectal cancer, or anything else.
- A male-dominated space promoting women's health to a male audience while never once telling that audience to take care of themselves.

The Halle Berry case:
- In June 2026, Berry told HuffPost that her perimenopause symptoms were always dismissed. Her comparison: "You didn't tell men that. They got Viagra."
- Berry's clinical experience — perimenopause misdiagnosed as herpes by her gynecologist — is a legitimate failure. Her individual story is documented and worth taking seriously.
- Her generalization is where the framing breaks. Berry takes one clinical encounter and extends it into a claim that men receive better treatment from the healthcare system. The population-level data runs the opposite direction. Viagra was developed by a pharmaceutical company pursuing a commercial market, not because the system prioritized men's sexual health.
- Berry's story is a numerator. One encounter, one dismissal, one patient. The argument this piece makes is about the denominator — who is in the system at all, and who the system was built to serve.

The broader campaign landscape:
- Breast cancer awareness dominates oncology fundraising and public consciousness.
- Movember is the only men's health awareness campaign with broad cultural penetration, and it doesn't approach the infrastructure behind pink ribbon marketing.
- Lung cancer kills more men than prostate cancer and gets even less campaign visibility.
- The Nightbirde Foundation (established in Movement 1) is one example of a pattern: cancer support organizations built on the premise that isolation during illness is a gendered problem women face, while men face cancer with less support infrastructure, less social permission to ask for help, and fewer organizations designed to reach them.

### MOVEMENT 3: THE INSTITUTIONAL NUMBERS

**Hospital-level programming (original analysis):**
- In 2017, Alam et al. surveyed the top 50 U.S. hospitals and found 98% had a women's health center while 32% had any kind of men's health center (PMID: 29264139). That study was never replicated or updated.
- Notes from the Abstract surveyed all 20 US News & World Report Honor Roll hospitals (2025-2026) in June 2026 and found the disparity persists: 100% (19 of 19) have a branded women's health center. 47% (9 of 19) have any kind of men's health center.
- Of those 9 men's health centers, only 3 (16% of Honor Roll hospitals) are truly multidisciplinary — offering primary care alongside three or more specialties in a coordinated program: NYU Langone (Preston Robert Tisch Center), UCLA (The Men's Clinic), and New York-Presbyterian (Iris Cantor Center, funded by a $20 million philanthropic gift).
- The remaining 6 are urology departments with expanded branding — treating erectile dysfunction, BPH, testosterone, and prostate conditions. Men's health gets reduced to a prostate and a penis. Women's health expanded beyond reproductive organs into whole-person care decades ago.
- The Iris Cantor Center at NYP is located in the same building as the Women's Health Center that the same donor established in 2002. The men's center opened more than twenty years later. One philanthropist had to write a $20 million check to create what the institution had built for women on its own.
- Source: Original analysis, Notes from the Abstract, June 2026. Dataset and methodology available on GitHub. Alam et al. 2017 (PMID: 29264139) for baseline comparison.

**The rural dimension:**
- The Honor Roll hospitals are all major urban academic medical centers. If the best-funded hospitals in the country have men's health programs at 47%, what does rural America look like?
- Roughly 1,353 Critical Access Hospitals serve rural America. They have 25 beds or fewer, operate on cost-based Medicare reimbursement, and struggle to keep their doors open. 152 rural hospitals have closed since 2010, including 52 CAHs.
- The chance that a CAH operates a dedicated men's health program is functionally zero. These facilities fight to maintain emergency services. Specialty programming for a population the equity framework doesn't recognize as underserved is not on the table.
- A 2024 Journal of Rural Health study (Chapel et al., USC Schaeffer Center) found that 60-year-old rural men now live two fewer years than urban men — a disparity that has nearly tripled over two decades. Rural men also spend 1.8 fewer years in quality health than urban men. For women, the rural-urban life expectancy disparity is six months. For men, it is two years.
- The ICAHN (Illinois Critical Access Hospital Network) said in June 2026: "Rural men are often less likely to seek preventive care and more likely to delay treatment until a health issue becomes serious."
- Higher rates of smoking, obesity, and cardiovascular conditions among rural men fuel the divide. The USC researchers concluded that by the time rural men reach 60, "there are limited opportunities to fully address this disparity, and earlier interventions may be needed."
- This is Dale Hammond's world. The system fails men at the population level, provides less infrastructure at the institutional level, and the failure intensifies in rural America where the need is greatest and the infrastructure is thinnest.
- Sources: Chapel et al., Journal of Rural Health, 2025 Jan;41(1):e12875. ICAHN press release, June 1, 2026. HRSA Rural Hospital Programs. Definitive Healthcare (1,353 CAH count).

**Federal infrastructure:**
- Five Offices of Women's Health exist at the federal level: CDC, FDA, HHS, HRSA, SAMHSA. Combined budgets in the tens of millions.
- The Office of Research on Women's Health within NIH has a $5 billion annual budget.
- Zero Offices of Men's Health exist anywhere in the federal government. Zero offices researching men's health. Combined budget: zero dollars.
- The Office on Women's Health was established in 1991. Thirty-five years later, no counterpart.
- Sources: AFRO, GM Today, Psychiatry Advisor, STAT News.

**Research funding:**
- NIH spends roughly $700 million annually on breast cancer research and $250 million on prostate cancer — almost three times more, despite comparable mortality.
- From 2013–2022, breast cancer received $8.36 billion in combined NIH and CDMRP funding versus $3.61 billion for prostate cancer.
- Federal cancer research funding correlates well with incidence (0.70) but poorly with mortality (0.36). Cancers that kill more get funded less when they lack advocacy infrastructure. The funding follows the awareness campaigns, not the body count.
- Sources: Fatherly (2021), Kamath et al. JCO/ASCO 2025, The Nuzzo Letter.

**Global reinforcement:**
- A 2024 WHO framework paper confirms that men "experience increased risk of morbidity and mortality across all ten major broad disease categories" and have "far fewer routine contact points with the healthcare system over their lifetimes than women."
- STAT News (March 2026): "Men's health is worse than women's across all socioeconomic and racial groups." This is not a demographic subset problem. It is universal.
- Source: Davis et al., "Mobile Wellness in the Workplace: Addressing the Global Men's Healthcare Gap," PMC (PMC11652426), December 2024. STAT News, March 12, 2026.

### MOVEMENT 4: THE BODY COUNT

**Life expectancy:**
- Male life expectancy in 2024: 76.5 years. Female: 81.4 years. A 4.9-year difference.
- STAT News (March 2026) reports the gap at 5.3 years, with men living "much shorter lives than their peers in other wealthy nations."
- The gap widened to 5.8 years in 2021 (largest since 1996), driven by COVID-19 and deaths of despair, both of which disproportionately affected men.
- Men die at an age-adjusted rate of 844.8 per 100,000 versus 613.5 for women — 37.7% higher.
- Sources: CDC/NCHS Data Brief No. 548 (January 2026), Harvard T.H. Chan (November 2024), STAT News (March 2026).

**Suicide — the gender paradox:**
- In 2024: 48,824 suicide deaths total. Male: 38,977. Female: 9,847. Four-to-one ratio.
- Males account for roughly 80% of all suicide deaths.
- Men over 75 face the highest suicide rates of any demographic group.
- The anticipated counterargument: women attempt suicide at higher rates (roughly 1.5 to 3 times more frequently). This is the "gender paradox in suicide," and the research on it strengthens the argument rather than weakening it.
- A cross-national study of 5,212 suicide attempts (Cully et al., EU-funded OSPI-Europe project) found that "Serious Suicide Attempts" were rated significantly more frequently in males than females (p < .001). Even within the same method — intentional drug overdose — serious intent was rated more often in men.
- In women, deliberate self-harm more often serves what the literature calls an "appeal function" — communicating distress or modifying the behavior of others. In men, deliberate self-harm correlates more strongly with suicidal intent. When self-harm appears in men, it more reliably predicts completed suicide.
- Male depression presents as "a distinct clinical phenotype challenging to recognize," meaning it gets missed, undertreated, and by the time suicidal behavior appears, the intent is lethal.
- The attempt-rate comparison overstates the equivalence. Men attempt less frequently, intend death more often, use more lethal methods, and have less intervention infrastructure catching them before they reach that point.
- Sources: AFSP (2024 data), SAVE, CDC/NCHS Data Brief No. 509, Cully et al. (ScienceDirect, gender paradox study), CAMS-CARE (2026), Springer (male depression phenotype chapter).

**Heart disease:**
- Leading cause of death for both sexes, but men develop it earlier and are 50% more likely to die from it.
- Source: BCBSM (April 2026).

**Psychiatric emergencies:**
- Men presenting to EDs for psychiatric emergencies: higher mortality (1.6% vs 0.7%), higher comorbidity, more likely to be uninsured (17.9% vs 12.5%).
- Men account for 80.3% of substance abuse/intoxication presentations and present with suicidal ideation at higher rates (6.7% vs 5.2%).
- Source: PMC (PMC11380797).

### MOVEMENT 5: THE DENOMINATOR

**The utilization disparity:**
- Women visit community health centers at almost double the rate of men: 445 per 1,000 women versus 249 per 1,000 men (CDC, 2022).
- Women are 33% more likely to visit a doctor. 100% more likely to seek preventive care, after controlling for age and removing pregnancy-related visits.
- Men are half as likely as women to seek medical care overall.
- Sources: Axios (October 2024), INTEGRIS/CDC, CDC/NCHS Data Brief No. 234.

**The women's clinical encounter problem reconsidered:**
- Women face documented problems inside clinical encounters: dismissed pain, undertreated cardiac symptoms, delayed autoimmune diagnoses. Berry's experience with perimenopause misdiagnosed as herpes belongs here. These are documented failures.
- Women have significantly more healthcare encounters per capita. More encounters means more opportunities for dismissal, misdiagnosis, and negative experiences. The raw volume of bad experiences is higher for women even if the per-encounter rate is comparable.
- The narrative that women are disproportionately mistreated in clinical settings is built on numerator data — counts of bad experiences — without adjusting for the denominator: women are in the room far more often.
- You cannot be gaslit by a doctor you never saw. You cannot have your symptoms dismissed at a clinic you never walked into.

**The equity framework's measurement failure:**
- The equity conversation selected on the population that showed up and ignored the population that didn't. From an epidemiological standpoint, this is selection bias baked into the equity measurement itself.
- Take the full population as the denominator rather than the people who sought care, and the picture inverts. Women have negative experiences inside the system. Men are not in the system. The system is failing men worse by every population-level measure.
- The equity framework looked at the exam room and saw women being failed. It did not look at the parking lot and notice men never pulled in.

### MOVEMENT 6: THE "CHOICE" PROBLEM

**The double standard in how public health treats underutilization:**
- When Black Americans underutilize mental health services, the field identifies barriers: cultural understanding, provider availability, workforce diversity, shared lived experiences. When rural populations present late, we call it an access problem and build outreach programs.
- When men underutilize care at half the rate of women, we call it stubbornness. We call it a choice.
- NIH defines health disparities as "largely preventable health differences that adversely affect populations who experience greater challenges to optimal health."
- NIMHD's designated disparity populations: racial and ethnic minorities, rural communities, sexual minorities, people with disabilities, low-SES populations.
- Men are not on that list. Despite dying 4.9 years earlier. Despite completing suicide at four times the rate. Despite utilizing care at half the rate. Despite having zero federal offices, zero dedicated research infrastructure, and 47% hospital program coverage versus 100% — with only 16% offering anything beyond urology.
- The framework was designed to identify populations the system is failing. It identified everyone except the one with the worst mortality outcomes.

### MOVEMENT 7: THE CALL FROM INSIDE THE HOUSE

**Which culture built the failing system:**
- Russell blames feminism. The culture responsible for the healthcare system's neglect of men is not feminist rhetoric. It is masculinity itself.
- The version of masculinity that says men are stoic, self-reliant, and don't need support is the same version that told hospital administrators men didn't need programs, told campaign designers men didn't need outreach, and told federal agencies men didn't need offices.
- That assumption predates the women's movement by centuries. Feminism didn't tell hospitals not to build men's health centers. The default understanding of manhood did. The people making those institutional decisions (many of them men) couldn't imagine men as a population that needed to be reached.
- The paradox at the center: the masculine identity many men value is the same identity that made the system decide they didn't need anything. The men who would most benefit from men's health infrastructure are the ones whose self-concept makes them least likely to demand it be built.
- The system built women's health centers, screening campaigns, branded programs, forty years of messaging that says "your health matters, come in, we built this for you." For men, the system built almost nothing, then documented that men don't seek care, don't get screened, and die earlier, as if those were characteristics of men rather than consequences of a system that never invited them.

### MOVEMENT 8: THE COUNTERARGUMENTS (woven through the piece, addressed directly)

**"Women were historically excluded from clinical trials."**
- True. The 1993 NIH Revitalization Act was necessary and important. Before 1990, "women's health" meant reproductive health, male mice dominated lab research, and the aspirin/heart attack study was conducted entirely on men.
- The correction worked. By FY 2014, women comprised 57.2% of NIH clinical research enrollment and 60% of Phase III trials. Women are no longer underrepresented. They are the majority. The infrastructure built to correct a historical wrong never pivoted to address the population now having worse outcomes.

**"The life expectancy gap is biological."**
- The gap varies from 1.3 years in Bahrain to 10.5 years in Russia. A boy born in Sweden lives 3.3 years less than a girl; in Russia, 10.5 years less. If biology were the sole driver, the gap would hold constant across societies and time. It does not.
- Cross-national variation proves structural and social factors are significant drivers. Lithuania's extreme male suicide rate followed post-Soviet economic disruption. South Korea's followed the 1997 Asian financial crisis. The U.S. gap widened during COVID. These are not biological events.
- Even if a biological component exists, that is an argument for more investment in men's health, not less. Women's higher autoimmune susceptibility justifies more autoimmune research, not indifference.

**"Women's health centers exist because of reproductive health needs."**
- Reproductive health justifies OB/GYN services. It does not justify the full scope of women's health center programming: cardiovascular screening, mental health, cancer prevention, nutrition, wellness.
- Of the 9 men's health centers at Honor Roll hospitals, 6 are urology departments with expanded branding. Men's health got reduced to urology. Women's health expanded into whole-person care decades ago. The reproductive argument proves the point: men's health never got the equivalent expansion.

**"Women have worse outcomes in specific clinical areas."**
- True in specific domains: maternal mortality, autoimmune disease, pain management, cardiac symptom dismissal.
- None of those domain-specific failures negate the population-level data. Men die at a 37.7% higher rate. Five-year life expectancy gap. Four-to-one suicide completion ratio with higher lethality of intent. Women face clinical encounter failures. Men face total infrastructure absence. Only one of these has federal offices, hospital programs at every top institution, and a funded advocacy ecosystem.

**"Women attempt suicide more often."**
- Addressed in Movement 4. The research shows men who attempt do so with higher lethality and greater intent to die. Deliberate self-harm in women more often serves a communicative function. Male depression presents as a distinct, harder-to-recognize clinical phenotype, meaning intervention fails before the attempt. The attempt-rate comparison without accounting for intent and lethality misrepresents the data.

**"Men should build their own advocacy movement."**
- We don't tell any other underserved population to build their own infrastructure.
- The State of Men's Health Act explicitly guarantees no money diverted from women's health. It uses existing funding. It has bipartisan support. Political fear of midterm optics has stalled it. The men's health movement cannot build what the political system won't allow to exist.

### MOVEMENT 9: THE PATH

**The State of Men's Health Act (H.R. 7602):**
- Bipartisan (Carter, D-LA; Murphy, R-NC). AUA-backed.
- No new entitlements. No new spending. Uses existing funding.
- Explicit guardrails: no money diverted from the Office on Women's Health.
- Stalled because of midterm optics, not policy objections.

**Reframe men's health as a family survival issue:**
- HHS found more than half of elderly women living in poverty were not in poverty before their husbands died. Men dying younger impoverishes wives and removes fathers.
- The women's health movement succeeded partly by connecting individual health to family and community consequences. Men's health has never made that case.

**Use the women's health infrastructure as the template, not the enemy:**
- Men's health advocates already say this: "The Office of Women's Health has done spectacular things. We've got a template to follow that's been incredibly successful."
- The Offices of Women's Health increased screenings, reduced deaths, decreased smoking, increased lifespan. An Office of Men's Health could produce the same benefits.

**Require sex-disaggregated outcome reporting in equity frameworks:**
- NIMHD's designated populations don't include men. Adding men doesn't remove anyone else. It forces the framework to measure what it has been ignoring.

**Build screening into the spaces men already occupy:**
- The NFL pink campaign proved male-dominated platforms can deliver health messaging at massive scale. That infrastructure has never been pointed at the audience.
- Workplaces, sporting events, trade unions, veteran organizations. Meet men where they are instead of expecting them to come to spaces the system built without them.
